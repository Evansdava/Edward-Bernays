Cigarettes

For women in the 1920's, smoking in public was taboo. George W. Hill, president of the American Tobacco Company, hired Bernays to open up the female market. Initially, Bernays marketed cigarettes as something of an alternative to food; to smoke instead of eating would keep you slim indeed! While this did boost sales, it didn't do anything about the taboo of public puffing. So, taking a different angle, Bernays consulted a psychoanalyst friend of his, Abraham Brill, who made the suggestion to associate cigarettes with "torches of freedom" in the feminism movement. Following this advice, Bernays had another friend of his organize a womens' rights march as part of the NYC Easter Day Parade, with a keystone feature of everyone lighting up cigarettes during it.
